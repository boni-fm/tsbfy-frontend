Are You Happy?

Alifia Savira Herawati
Slice of Life

It was bleak. And plain. And boring. Not like she cared about it, though. It has just started to get very annoying and pathetic, if she could be honest. The thing was, she couldn't even be honest to her friends about this. What was it? Loneliness? If that was dramatic, boredom? Whenever she was being asked, her answer remained the same.

"I have my own happiness."

Her friends would roll their eyes, "and that is?"

She would give them her K-Pop idols' merchandise in front of their faces, or talk about her favorite book's characters for hours. And they sighed, loudly even.

"You know, isolating yourself will lead to frustration."

The first time she heard it, she was stunned because she didn't think it was an act of isolation. But as time passed by, she could understand their point of view.

"It's not a form of isolation. It's just me setting proper boundaries."

"Well, human's nature is to socialize in real life with each other. Do tell us, are you satisfied by just gratifying yourself with fiction? Are you genuinely happy with that?"

Those were the regular questions she didn't dare to answer every time. She would just shrug or snort before changing the subject. And this always happened like a circle.

She found it ironic that her life lately is being determined by a cycle of tiredness built in her mind, self, and feelings. She couldn't get rid of her unknown annoyance that grew even bigger.

It's not like she was gradually deteriorating or vanishing from her real life. She just felt empty and pointless in this state. She's been working from day to night, doing work, doing homework, and doing anything normal for a human being according to her beliefs. But why, why indeed.

It felt helpless.

But alas, the Lord is caring, loving, and merciful. He surely won't make his creatures feel hopeless for too long, so he sent a saviour for this poor woman. A saviour in which she almost detested at the first glance.

—

Socialising wasn't really her thing. In fact, she avoided it most of the time, which is probably one of her sources of the existential problems mentioned before. She didn't hate it, almost, maybe. But not a certainty. Besides, hate was a strong word. She just performed better without having to do it.

At least that's what she thought until her one close friend offered a position in a short event.

"Come on. It won't last long! Probably just for two or three weeks," persuaded her friend. She wasn't contemplated enough, but it sure raised her interest. Two or three weeks only, including its preparation. It was really short.

"I doubt it. It requires working under pressure with so many strangers. It must be very stressful." She tried to reject and deny that she was already slightly intrigued.

"Oh yeah, it is. But it won't be so bad. I couldn't guarantee if it would entertain you or not. I'm a hundred percent sure it will be meaningful, at least for someone who dislikes crowds like you because it involves dealing with books."

She paid her attention fully to her friend. "What kind of event?" She asked. Her friend's face lit up for a second.

"Volunteer. Aaaaaand you don't have to prepare too much. Just say yes, and you'll be assigned to the distribution committee immediately." She raised her eyes, signaling the opposite woman to explain more.

"Uh, what else to say? I genuinely think that it would be great to try. Sure, we will not be paid but volunteering for others who need our help, why not, right?"

"You could define distributing things— with books, perhaps?"

And her friend understood. She began to hear about the vision of this event. Its expected results as her interest and excitement grew bigger.

"Alright, it's enough. I understand." She said, for the first time since a very long time, she could feel her chest beat faster than normal.

"I'll join the committee."

—

It took several years through that one unexpected event for her to realize that she liked teaching. She even started to feel that this was a hidden passion that her brain decided not to recognize. She almost felt like she wasted her time to think about her stresses, which led to dreads from her self-proclaimed boredom by isolation several nights before.

"You are surprisingly very good with the kids!" They said when they were also incredible and even better with children at several points. The small "ah, not really, but thank you." That sentence was always murmured afterward by her. Sometimes, she could sense that her face became hot, and she felt bashful for a second.

"They are right, y'know," her friend, who brought her to this situation, said. "You're so good at teaching too. What did you prepare with the distribution team?"

"You said I was just going to distribute books and introduce them briefly to the kids. I did what I had to do, but they were asking for more information, so I gave it to them."

"Mmm, did you enjoy it?" Bingo. Right on the bull's eyes.

She stopped momentarily. Thinking about what she should say about it. There was no point in lying or acting. She could be honest. She could admit it went well with her boundaries. She could say that this truly relieves her desperation to be free and content.

But neither of that felt true enough at that time. She felt like it was too soon to say all those things when she just barely realised it at the moment. It was new for her, at least for her in this early adult livelihood. It needs to be studied and analysed before she could confidently confirm that—

Or it's just her form of anxiety. She mentally noted it to her brain, post-production of her boredom and tiredness.

Whatever it was, she gave her regular attitude instead in response with a petit refreshment that she rarely gave.

"Probably almost as much as liking my idols," she smiled a little and continued before her friend could react, "Socialising is not a bad thing, I guess when you start it with things you like. It went well with my boundaries."

There was a pause before her friend shouted happily, "I knew it! It was almost according to you. But for me, you know, you ought to be more confident in yourself. You are good with people, just not knowing how to start and think that it is normal to not communicate with real people for a long time because your work is arresting you!"

It was a revelation for her. Her friend was partially, perhaps, fully right about it.

"Is it?" She asked. The other smiled gently.

"Look at the kids with the books you've explained. They're happy that you socialized it for them, and they also get new knowledge."

She placed her gaze on the kids that were gleefully exchanging conversations about the books that were just being distributed and explained by her. It was almost like watching a mirage, no? That expression was far too deviated. It felt like watching her idol's documentary, who was excited about new things outside their career's life that were being introduced by their fans and regular people around them.

It made her feel enthusiastic, as if she could feel so many vibrant colors gushing through her soul and mind.

It was terrifying. Thrilling. Exciting. If she had to confirm things, she would confirm those senses.

"Say, honestly," her friend's voice brought her back to consciousness. She looked directly into her eyes while glancing at the afar scenery.

"Are you happy?"

With that, she thought about the doomed cycle she's been into and thought that she probably could understand how to fix her suffering.

"I am."
