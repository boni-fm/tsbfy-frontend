Dancers from Oleh Sari

Idetha Putri Kurnia
Slice of Life

"This is the end," Linga muttered to himself when he saw his reflection in the mirror, which was half his height. He bent down to attach a crown decoration to his head. People used to call it omprok. The headdress was made of buffalo skin decorated with gold and red ornaments, which match the makeup.

He complimented the makeup on his face, "Now there is no difference between the other dancers, except..." he suddenly remembered the Simbok incident yesterday.

I was sweeping sheep dung in the cage that afternoon when suddenly I heard Simbok's scream from inside, shouting my name. I immediately ran into the house and looked for Simbok. I was afraid that something might happen.

"Mbok, where is Simbok?" I looked for her in the kitchen, but she wasn't there, she wasn't even in the room, and I saw a shadow in my room. Simbok was there, in my room crying, and I saw my secret in his grasp. I walked over to him, kneeling on his lap.

"I'm sorry, Mbok." I held back my sobs. I knew Simbok was disappointed. Simbok gripped my secret tighter, sad and disappointed with me like this. Cried and sobbed reverberate in this small bamboo room. Simbok's disappointment with what I did fell through tears and sobs. The last sob I heard from Simbok muffled the echo in this small room. Simbok stopped crying and tried to stand up, letting go of my hand on his lap. Simbok tried to stand even though the waves had hit her with imperfect footing.

Simbok said, "Simbok is disappointed with you." Simbok's tears broke for the second time.

She continued, "what would your father do if he found out about this, Son?" Simbok's sobs became more intense. I crawled, holding Simbok's feet tightly, crying at Simbok feet.

"I'm sorry, Mbok. I know it was wrong. Sorry, Mbok," I burst into tears when Simbok pulled his leg and threw the mask on the ground.

"Get rid of all this, don't let you know." Simbok left my room with disappointment still in his heart.

In the evening, the three of us had dinner in silence. Thankfully, Simbok's eyes were no longer red, but I could see a few bruises on his eyes. I knew Simbok kept crying in his room after scolding me earlier. I heard it when I packed up my things. Even though Simbok tried to keep her voice down, this little house couldn't catch the sound of Simbok's crying. The Simbok I knew for her stubbornness broke with the sharp crash of the waves. Father's voice broke the atmosphere and started the conversation.

"How was your school, Son?"

"It's fine, Father."

"You will graduate soon, don't be like me and Simbok, who didn't graduate from elementary school. In the past, I went to school, passed five villages, and walked ten kilos every day. Cough... cough..." Father stopped the conversation.

Simbok rushed to give him a drink, "When you eat, don't say anything, sir. It's going to be awkward." Simbok rubbed father's back, trying to relieve his cough.

"Cough... already... already, Mbok." We continued this dinner in silence. No one continued the earlier conversation or started a new one. Simbok and father have entered the room because it is already ten o'clock at night. Usually, I sneaked out for dance practice at the Pak Dhe Tuha Studio. However, I was not there to practice dancing but to say goodbye.

...

Blarongan Studio

Village By Sari

The chanting after chanting of the drums sounded beautiful and brought this studio to life. This chant could make the heart move and create beautiful moves. I saw Pakde teaching children my age to dance the Jaranan Buto dance.

Pakde saw me and called my name, "come here, Lingg. Just come in."

I did not go to the line but approached Pakde, who was sitting in his chair not far from the line of dancers.

"Thank you, sir. Linga is not here to dance, but here." I handed over the buto mask he lent me and the red sampur he gave me since I studied here.

"That's fine then, Son. Sir, take the mask. The sampur is for you." Pakde took the buto mask and put it on the ground.

"From the start, Pakde knew that your parents didn't approve of you dancing, but you have talent, Son. Pakde has forbidden you from coming here, but you insisted on dancing. Yes, I, let it be." Pakde stopped and looked at me, who bent down on his lap. He stroked my head, "Pakde knows you really like dancing. Dancing has become a part of your life as breathing. Pakde is willing to let go of a talented child like you."

I said goodbye for the last time. I really didn't want to let go of all this. All my dreams were here. I wanted to be like Pakde, who became a dancer, teaching dance to future successors. So that what Pakde had done was not in vain in the end. I wanted to open a studio and teach Indonesian dance to the next generation of this nation. I wanted to, but why? Why should I give up all this? Why didn't Simbok and father support me like other parents? Was it because I was a man? Was it because I shouldn't dance like a woman? Why? Why was I born a boy? WHY?!

All the way out of the studio, I held back these tears. I held back my anger. For the last time, I stood in this studio.

...

Banyuwangi was where I was born and where I grew up with its unique culture. Banyuwangi was famous for its various regional dances that attract foreign tourists. In the past, studios like Pakde's studio became a busy place. Many studios were standing in Banyuwangi. In my village, we only had Sanggar Blarongan, which one uncle owned. Along with the time, the more forgotten folk dances. In the past, Pakde said, there were more than five studios in the village of By Sari, but only Pakde's studios have survived to this day.

Tringgg...

The long bell rang, signaling the end of today's lesson. My friends flocked out of the classroom after the teacher said hello. I, who was still tidying up the books, listened to the conversations of my friends, Ajeng and Yanti.

"Then don't forget to practice dancing in the field, Yan. You brought the sampur, right?" Said Ajeng.

"Yes, I brought it," answered Yanti. In my heart, I wondered, 'do they want to practice dancing? Isn't there nothing happening in Bakungan village? I ventured to ask them.'

"What are you dancing to?" I asked, snatching their conversation.

Ajeng and Yanti were surprised to hear me asking them. "Oh, it's you, Ling. We are dancing for the Gandrung Sewu festival later."

Gandrung Sewu needs a lot of participants, I thought. "When is the event?" I asked again.

"About two more months, Ling. The brochure is on the wall, and there will be a selection before..." Ajeng hadn't finished her conversation yet, but I had already started running towards the wall magazine.

"Thank you, Jeng."

"That's it," I said to myself. I saw a red flyer that was pasted. A brochure that stated, 'Gandrung Sewu Festival, looking for 1000 participants, register your best participants'.

"I promise this is the last time," I mumbled softly as I gripped the flyer tightly.

...

I stopped at the Blarongan, Pakde's studio. I wanted to show him this brochure.

"Sir, I, I want to dance at this festival. For the last time," I said excitedly. Pakde, who was dancing, stopped. He saw me coming and shouted at him, tired of running. He sat down and called me to sit beside him.

"Come here, Ling." He put the buto mask beside him and patted my shoulder, "are you sure? You know the consequences?"

I looked at Pakde sharply with my ambition, "Lingga is sure, Pakde. Lingga will be responsible for the consequences. Lingga just wants Pakde to teach Lingga this Gandrung Sewu dance." I knelt down, "please, sir, one last time."

Pakde stroked my head and said, "okay, come here tonight and bring your sampur. We practice. Change your clothes, don't wear school clothes. You'll get dirty."

I couldn't hide my happy tears. I held my uncle's hand. "Thank you, sir. Thank you. Linga has to go first." I was so happy that I didn't want to let go of Pakde's hand.

"Enough, already. Go home, help Simbok."

I replied cheerfully, "Okay, sir."

That night at Sanggar Blarongan, I felt a strange thing. Unlike usual, this night was quiet. Not a single drum or gamelan could be heard. I saw Pakde alone in the middle of the field and a xylophone beside him. I called him cheerfully, "Sir."

Pakde looked at me, "Come here, Ling."

Unable to bear it, I asked him something strange that I felt, "Pakde, why aren't there any children? No one plays drums and gamelan either."

Pakde replied calmly, "it is specific for you to practice. Pakde wants to focus on training you."

I nodded silently.

Pakde continued, "You know, in the past, the dancers of the Gandrung Sewu dance were men who were groomed like women."

I replied, "Lingga knows, sir." I continued, "and now, men don't dance this dance anymore. All were replaced by women."

Before I spoke again, the sir looked at me. "But dancing is not just a woman's job. Men can dance too. Dance whatever they like, say anything with beautiful dance moves. Dance with your heart. That's the most important thing." Pakde put his hand on my chest, "get ready!"

This is the time for me to be me. Pakde taught me slowly. Until I knew one move. Then he would play the xylophone, and I danced.

...

My daily routine kept repeating itself. After I went home from school, I carried goats, cleaned the cage, helped Simbok clean the house, and at night had dinner together in this bamboo hut. When Simbok had put out the candles at home, I sneaked out of the house and went to dancing practice at the Pakde Studio until the day of selection came. Fortunately, the selection day was held on Sunday. Pakde took me to Baungan village. There we were selected. Luckily, Pakde had an acquaintance there, Mr. Besro. At first, I was not allowed to join the selection because I was a boy, but fortunately, after Pak Besro saw me dancing, he changed his mind. I passed the selection. I was happy. Of course, Pakde asked Pak Besro to judge me in the room, where the other dancers couldn't see me. I danced like Pakde taught me, being myself for the umpteenth time.

On the way home, I repeatedly thanked Pakde. On this old motorbike, I felt happy while enjoying the natural beauty of Banyuwangi, which was starting to darken. We arrived at the village of Oleh Sari. I said goodbye when I arrived at the Blarongan Studio. When I got home, I saw a lot of people in my house. The neighbors called me, "son, hurry up, your father!"

Father? Father, why? I ran into the house and went to my mother who was crying in her room. I saw my father lying on the bed, his breath panting as he had run around a village. Mother cried beside him. Aware of my arrival, she ran to weep in my body. With his eyes weakened, father looked at me as if he wanted to say something. "Ling... according to Simbok's words..." Before he finished his last words, he breathed his last. Simbok's tears broke, and so did I.

...

We had to sell our goats to pay for his tahlilan. Since my father died, Simbok had taken care of the rice fields. I always wanted to go with Simbok to the rice fields in the morning, but Simbok always forbade me. Simbok always told me to go to school. However, Simbok seemed tired of nagging me because today, Simbok was silent and didn't scold me for school. I couldn't bear seeing Simbok working hard while I didn't help her in the fields. Taking care of the fields was a tough job. Simbok must have been tired because she was often sick. That was why it became irregular, and I often replaced her in the fields.

The days that passed made me realize that I had to bury my dream of becoming a dancer. I had to replace you and take care of Simbok. It was getting late. I had to get ready to go home. Simbok was also sick, and I couldn't leave her too long. I was ready to go home, and I saw my idol, Pakde Tuha.

He came up to me and asked how I was doing, "are you okay, Ling?" My tears almost spilled. I had failed. I failed to achieve my dream. I was disappointed, sir. I couldn't stand it. I kneel to apologize to him.

"Nyuwun pangapunten, sir. I'm sorry. Linga has failed." Pakde asked me to get up.

"Get up, Ling. Get up." Pakde held my hand, asking me to stand up straight, "Lingga, now you have to be strong, stand up straight, take care of your Simbok and stay who you are."

When we got home, we had dinner together. It seemed that Simbok's condition had improved. After eating, I wanted to massage Simbok's feet, but Simbok said she was healthy, and the next day, she would go to the fields again. I was lying in my room. I remembered what Pakde said. Keep being you. I realized who I was. I realized I wasn't being myself and became someone else I didn't know. I seemed to borrow someone else's empty figure. I closed my eyes, and when I opened them again, I promised myself, "This is my last servant for you and me." I checked the date stuck in my closet. There were only two days left for the Gandrung Sewu celebration to be held. I met Pakde that night and told him my promise. Dancing at the festival will be the last dance.

...

That day. Yes, that day was the day that the festival was held. I said goodbye to Simbok. My heart was racing because I felt guilty to Simbok for lying with enthusiasm to fulfill my dream one last time, "I'm sorry, Mbok. Forgive your son. This is the last time."

In front of this small mirror, I lament this sinful self.

"I know, I'm not a dutiful child. I'm a sinner, really. But please give me one last chance to be who I am."

...

After the festival, I went about my days as usual. Simbok was rarely sick because I always helped her in the fields, so she didn't get tired anymore. Simbok was never angrily telling me to go to school again. Every day I always accompanied her to the fields. Since our father's death, our economic condition had declined, so we had to work hard to be able to eat day after day. I thought Simbok wouldn't talk about my education issues, but tonight Simbok did.

"Son, I'm sorry. Because of me, you can't continue your school."

I immediately replied, "Simbok doesn't need to apologize. Lingga is okay, Mbok. Lingga prefers to help Simbok in the fields."

"Son, it looks like Simbok can't go to the fields anymore. Simbok will tell people to take care of our fields. Later we will share them," said Simbok.

"Simbok, no need. Linga can take care of his fields, Mbok," I said softly.

Simbok stroked my head, "Simbok got news from Pak Le that you are in Jakarta. He said he asked you to work in his workshop." Simbok continued the conversation, "Simbok will order a ticket to Jakarta. Just get ready."

I was shocked by Simbok's words. How could I leave Simbok here alone? "But then, who will look after Simbok when Lingga goes to Jakarta?" I asked worriedly.

"Simbok will be fine here. Simbok doesn't take care of the fields anymore. So don't worry."

Half-heartedly I replied, "Okay, Mbok. Lingga will go to Jakarta." I couldn't bear it if I didn't comply with Simbok's request. I didn't want to be disobedient a second time.

Today I went to Jakarta. In the beauty of the rice fields that I continued to pass, I also missed all my dreams that were carried by the wind that greeted me away. A dream that was born in Banyuwangi, and must also be buried in this land.
