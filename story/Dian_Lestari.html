Acacia

Dian Dwi Lestari
Slice Of Life

I always woke up at 6 am, just as my alarm went off. Sometimes I woke up early, but I started my activities after hearing the alarm. It has been set up that way, at least these last three years. Oliver arranged it for me.

The first thing I did was watering the acacia seedlings near the fence. Oliver said he purposely put them there to get enough sun. Yeah, that was a gift from Oliver. He said that I had the enthusiasm to do activities in the morning, and when the tree got big, we could see it together. I snorted after hearing it but still watered it every morning. My part was only watering. Oliver did the rest, like changing the soil, fertilizer, and moving it to a larger field. It used to be only as high as my thighs, but then it felt a little taller than me. I could feel the leaves hitting the corners of my eyes every time I got close. I was going to ask Oliver to put a hose on so I could make it easier to water.

“Done with the tree, Myles?”

Oh, that was Oliver. I could feel him standing next to me before he closed the fence.

“As usual,” I reply.

He led me to walk inside.

“Just leave the buzzer there,” he said. We went to the terrace for breakfast.

“Is salmon good for breakfast? I woke up a bit late, thought of grilling the salmon for a bit and making instant mashed potatoes,” he said as he arranged the food, I guess.

“Not bad,” I replied.

Mornings were always like this, Oliver came before work to have breakfast together. Actually, he didn’t need to bother like this. Just left out some instant or frozen food, and I could warm it myself up in the microwave.

He came at least three times a day to eat together at lunchtime. He also came after work, of course. He said the hospital where he worked was quite far away but insisted on coming anyway. I couldn’t get rid of him either, could I?

Yes, he worked at the hospital. More specifically, a doctor. The doctor I met after an accident three years ago. He was kind enough to help me after I lost sight in the accident. That was how our friendship started until now.

“I have a surgery scheduled for this afternoon. I will send lunch delivery from a restaurant near the hospital.” He said that as he cleared the table.

Yes, of course, there were times like this. When he had surgery during the day, he would deliver food for me. Not a big deal, of course. Usually, the delivery guy would put the rice box on the terrace. I picked it up after getting a call from Oliver.

“Forgot to tell you something. I think the tree needs to be moved. Isn’t it already getting taller? Also, I seem to need a hose to water it more easily.”

He was silent for a moment, and seemed to be observing the tree.

“Okay, we’ll do it the day after tomorrow. I don’t have any schedule. I’ll go first, son.”

Well, technically, our age gap was quite far. Maybe five or seven years? Of course, I had never seen his face and never asked his age, only guessing how old the doctor who would finish his specialization program. But it felt weird to be called that by someone I had never seen.

Oliver was like a friend and a parent because I had been living alone for so long. My mother’s husband left us when I was little, and my mother became a drunkard. I spent several years with my mother before my aunt decided to take care of me and send me to school. Then I was accepted at the University in this city and decided to start living on my own, continuing my studies while doing part-time jobs.

I would never forget the accident. I remembered clearly what the face of the bespectacled man sitting behind the wheel looked like. I heard from my aunt that he escaped prison and chose to pay the fine. Of course, he must be wealthy.

Oliver said it was difficult to find eye donors in our city, but he kept trying to find eye donors from the central eye bank.

I wanted to get my sight back. I used to think I’d meet that guy after I got the eye donor and punched him at least once, but it was all over the place. Maybe it was because of that bad intention I didn’t immediately get an eye donor. I wanted to go back to college and get on with my life. Maybe I wanted to be a doctor like Oliver. I also wanted to see his face and thank him. Then the acacia tree in the yard, I wanted to see it too.

Oliver came a bit late that day because he didn’t have a schedule.

“I woke up late,” he said. As we promised the other day, we would move the acacia tree to the ground, no more pots because it was quite big. We started moving it after breakfast. I was not much help, only when Oliver told me to slide or hold something. We also installed a hose to make it easier to water the tree.

I could imagine the tree now. It must be standing tall in the garden without a pot. You don’t think about a house with a narrow yard. The yard was grass with a wooden fence. That house belonged to Oliver, and he lived in my apartment at the moment. In short, we swapped places. He said it was better that way so that I wouldn’t be confined in a small room and get lots of fresh air. I didn’t know what this house looked like, but I thought it’s quite big. I once asked why he didn’t live there with me, since that house belonged to him. He said that my apartment needed someone to look after. Tell me, was there anyone kinder than Oliver? I owed him a lot.

“Good news from the central corneal bank. I have resubmitted them, and you got the first queue.” He spoke while hugging my shoulder and shaked it lightly.

“Of course, that's good news.” I replied with a smile.

I got excited to hear that. I wished I could have my eyesight again, and I hoped this time luck was on my side.

And luck was really on my side. Oliver came rushing one afternoon between hours when we had just finished lunch an hour ago. But I heard the gate open rather loudly, and he started shouting my name before he even entered. I waited a while for him to catch his breath. To be honest, I was getting nervous too. I felt like I knew what this was, but my mind was churning, refusing to make any small hopes, but I was very, very sure. I gently squeezed the pants I was wearing.

“What’s wrong? Didn’t you just come here?” I decided to start the conversation.

“Yes... right. I mean... this is a job... this is also a job... I... God !! You get a donor, Myles! You got it!!” He shaked my body.

I felt my soul leaving my body. I couldn’t believe that. Was that true? I had been waiting for this moment for two years, and it felt unreal. I was getting very, very nervous. The first thing that popped into my head was Oliver. He was the person I wanted to see the most for the first time. I was really nervous. Would this work?

We immediately rushed to the hospital. I also called my aunt, and she was so happy about it, I heard her almost scream. She said she would be here soon. I did some checks, not too many, because I also routinely did check-ups to see the state of my eyes. Again, it was Oliver as my guardian, who suggested that. He said it would make the procedure to get a donor easier.

I was really nervous when I was about to enter the surgery room. My aunt has arrived here, and Oliver was also with my aunt. I didn’t know why I was really nervous. Even though I couldn’t see it, I could feel my aunt and Oliver’s nervousness and it made me even more nervous. Everything would go smoothly, right?

I hadn’t seen Oliver since I woke up from surgery. It had been three weeks, and my eyesight was getting better. My aunt said he had urgent matters out of town. I was a little disappointed, but still I couldn’t see very well either, so I thought I would see them when my eyesight was perfect. My aunt had been accompanying me for the past three weeks because I still had to go to the doctor for checkups until I could really see clearly. I could already see what the acacia tree planted in the yard looked like. It was really taller than me. Many things turned out to be far different from what I expected. For example, Oliver’s house. I thought it was brick red like farm houses in general, but it turned out to be much more beautiful with similar shades of white and greenery all around the house. I wondered how Oliver took care of it all by himself before me because, hey, I was only just finding out now!

But I actually gradually recovered pretty quickly. I could see clearly without glasses, and my eyes didn’t water much anymore. That day would be my last check-up, but Oliver never showed himself at all. I didn’t even have his cell number. The doctor said I could check up again next month, which means I was 99% recovered. My Aunt and I bought some bread before we headed home. Seeing the red light, I was reminded of the accident again. But I thought I had forgotten it. I really didn’t want to remember it anymore because I felt happier then. I felt I could be more grateful for many things. Until the moment we crossed the fence, I saw him.

The man with glasses, whom I saw three years ago, was standing on the terrace of the house, looking straight at me. I was even cursing myself for just saying I didn’t want to remember that accident but thinking of his face instead. But that face was totally real! I almost lost my sanity, even ignoring my Aunt, who tried to put her arm around my shoulder. I walked briskly towards him, with my eyes starting to feel warm. My emotions were really out of control.

I quickly grabbed his collar and looked at his face. It was that face! The face I last saw three years ago and one that I would always remember. I raised my hand and was about to punch him until he started talking.

 “Hello, Myles!”

That voice! Oliver.
