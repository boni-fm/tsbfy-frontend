Ticking Time That Lingers

Deandra Alika Hefandia
Romance

When did it started, she wonders. To be frank, she doesn’t really remember either. She doesn’t remember how or when it started, or how she got here. The fragments of the memories are probably laying carelessly on the floor and it feels somewhat draining to piece them back into one. At least, she remembers how she came up to the door of her crib, accepting an envelope she was sure sent from no one—not anybody she was reminded of, who still sends letter these days? Unless, it wasn’t a letter and she found out it was a wedding invitation.

It only adds up of why she ended up here now, sitting on the corner of a small, humble café in the not-so-crowded part of the city but it sure was filled with a lot of things. Funnily, it should’ve been enough to shoo her away, but it didn’t because she was there and the scenes of the days counted until this very moment was moving in flashback like a film reel inside her head. The small café sure hold its own charm and she was willing to let it kept her encaged in it until she thought it was a bit too much of a nostalgia to stay over something so petty or quite irrelevant until a few weeks earlier when she accepted the damned envelope.

The answer came right after she opened the broken-white envelope, it didn’t look so clean, being inside the bag of other letters and stuff. It didn’t excite her one bit and the name written on the invitation didn’t make it better. She wondered if it was fever dream because if she remembered things correctly, then it has been a few years since she saw the name. Probably, up until the graduation of college and she decided to leave things behind. It feels like a long prolog into a very old story that even herself hadn’t touched, not sure why either, maybe it’s because she subconsciously dug a hole deep enough to bury the memories or she just decided that it wasn’t worth thinking about, or maybe it stayed in the back of her head sometimes but she was unwilling to accept it.

The things she decided not to pursue nor hold on any further.

Like, a cheesy, teeny crush or the fear of failing the class, for example. In all honesty, it was cheesy and it was the best she could ever summarize the situation. A sophomore year until the she was preparing for her graduation, it was a cliché crush on your same circle friend and as cliché as it could be, it was well hidden and still locked up tight in a chest somewhere between the space of her memory and heart. Who wouldn’t, honestly? He wasn’t a bad man and was definitely better compared to the rest, it wasn’t her fault either that such weird persona could give her a sense that the person was untouchable, at least by her. It was quite cute if she was able to tell the tales without a bitter bite on her tongue, in reality she just couldn’t and she just refused to open that secret, not even to her old friends that still kept in touch with her even now.

It was quite of a normal college anecdote and most likely, that was how she ended up spending the rest of her college years. Hanging out with her friends, struggling with tests, and leaving out at night after a long final exam week. It was normal and all. To think about it, she knew she was quite close to him and as much as she protected the lines, she knew it might only be a huge façade she created. After all, the consequences of having a crush on a friend from the same group most of the time ended in doom. If it’s not the relationship, then the friendship had to be buried six feet under, not everybody was destined to have the romance genre movies ending. She knew that well and better than anybody, probably it was just a smart excuse to hide the cowardice inside of her because if she ever got the chance and guts to live to tell the story, she would, but she didn’t and that was on her.

That was how she ended up staring into blank space in a good five minutes in her car before she could gather her courage to step out of the vehicle and enter the venue. Each of her footsteps was filled and heavy with worry and unthinkable amount of questions. It was the same hesitancy because she didn’t know whether she should feel this weird or whether she should feel her chest tightening each time she got closer to the big, broad door to the hall. It was years ago. An unfinished matter—but it was years ago, she didn’t have to stumble on her own mind like that. If it wasn’t for a loud greeting of her old friends, she probably would’ve dwell on the same cycle of thoughts for the rest of the wedding. She remembered her old friend whispering to her about how she thinks they made such a good couple—the bridge and the groom. She could only be relieved when her friend didn’t catch that wistful smile she had for a very short seconds on her face when they greeted her and joking on how the turntables of life for them to see another one of their friend got married.

“It’s been a while!” they said. “It’s been years.” She replied with a chuckle before the group started to gather and share what they have been up to. Some already found their significant others and some has been around for quite a while, some changed a lot, both appearance-wise or personality-wise. She noted on how a few years could really change a lot of things and the meeting was both familiar and strange. A part of her could see the people that she used to hang with, one that wore glasses and reminded them about the curfew, some others who looked quite free-spirited back in the days now seemed to be more courteous about themselves. It changed but also didn’t because they were practically the same people, just went through a different narrative of adulting up until then, still do even when this wedding party is over later. It was the exact same thing when she faced the groom in the party to congratulate them. It felt forced, but was she happy to see him well, it felt like a false, good-girl-selfless-faith, because the awkwardness was only on her and it lingered.

It wasn't her fault that she liked him nor was it her fault to stopped doing so when she found out about his own crush at one, quiet night when their friends were getting their foods and she was left to wait at the table with him. That was actually quite a night. The group decided they should visit a beach before the semester was over and she agreed because it felt like a nice breeze in the middle of her college year struggle. She probably could still talk about the sky being quite clear, they were all talking while sitting on the sand, talking about whatever, it was fun. The sky was pretty, it was full moon—not the best time to be near a beach, honestly, but it complimented the moment quite beautifully because it was a piece of her youth. Early 20s and pursuing dreams, hand-in-hand in being realistic at the same time.

So standing here now, lending her hand out and faux-smiling at them, feeling gruesomely bitter at her own head was truly a moment she’d love to skip over. They greeted her first and for some reason, the bride still remembered her and she remembered who she were. That was when she was fully aware on how self-centered and selfish it sounded even in her own head to even feel bad about something so holy like an occasion of tying the knot between the two people. Thus she shook his hand, gave him some formalism congratulatory words before she left the hall.

Anyhow, it was strange, even when she drove away from that place and aimlessly wandering around the town. Honestly should an old crush affect her this much? She wouldn’t know herself and she knew she couldn’t get an answer from her friends, they never knew about it anyway. She was good at hiding it and her friends were not world’s most sensitive person, though it was okay for her.

She drove past the familiar street where she used to hang with her college friends back in the days. It looked both the same and different. She still see the same, old telephone box that they didn’t get rid of and she could still easily point out the corner of the street where they used to place a bench on—the one that they did get rid of now. She saw a new café were opening too along the street and she saw some newly built restaurants. Now the street felt more like a district than an empty path, it was the charm to it but she didn’t hate it either now. The new things filled the empty space and she preferred that, it was easier for her heart.

Now in a whole different part of the city where she sits, her eyes linger out of the window and the fog of her coffee is no longer there from being left untouched. She remembers how the friendship bloomed and what it takes for her to cut the line off for her own sake. The scene should’ve been familiar and she knows what happened well enough to not do the same thing again, it keeps rewinding over and over inside her brain, God knows how long would it take for her to stop. It stopped for years until now. The reason is clearly coming from her digging her own grave of memories, the ones she buried alive.

The time ticks tad a bit slower for now when she’s staring into her drink and the rain is pitter-patters outside of the café. She doesn’t notice the sky even turned dark until now. It’s quite absurd for her to ponder over something like this and it’s even weirder that she lets herself doing so, feeding on her own gloom for the moment and refuses to move on because well, everybody did. They moved on and are in a better places in their lives, not saying that she’s not in a great place but the attachment to old things sure can be one hell of a drag onto her ankles like shackles.

In moments like these where she reconsiders her decisions, it’s only humane to see her usual nonchalant side starts to get very rowdy and making up long scenarios of what-ifs and what-not. Because there would be a ton of other choices of how a story could unfold if she had chosen one different option that she didn’t. Like how she cusses her own fear and her tendency to look away from what she wants because she’s scared of the outcome, right before she even tried to dip a toe in.

Like, what could be the worst scenario if that night she just blatantly told him how cool he looked to her and how she admired him? What could be so bad in telling the person she liked throughout her study before she graduated and had to change to adapt into a whole new environment? What could’ve gone so wrong if back then she could’ve just told him everything because now, nothing seemed to be that different from the outcomes she had calculated. They didn’t keep in touch, they didn’t even meet each other until today. If she did what she should have, it would’ve ended up the same, probably just not attending the wedding and was it really worth the years of staying as quiet as the mountain?

As of now, probably she should just shakes it off of her mind. She huffs a small smile along with a scoff at her own naivety and stupidity. It’s an old crush, she notes to herself. The time stops for the whole week to remind her of her unfinished business and she ought to learn how to forget and pass on now. It’s almost funny that she actually got this far just to give up on her own bitterness that she never managed to properly touch until today.

And maybe those thoughts will linger only for today, just until the rain stops. When it stops, the white cloth of her table would’ve been clear of her traces and the time will tick again. It will not stop, not anytime soon. Maybe by then learning and letting go would be a fact than a telltale she held on. Then maybe her chapter will meet a prologue, then maybe the what-ifs wouldn’t matter so much anymore.
